Why this matters

By sharing identical assessments, we can directly compare learning outcomes. The only systematic difference is the teaching approach — your AI-enhanced web app vs. Rolf's traditional methods.

Three Research Questions

RQ1: Learning Outcomes

Does the AI-integrated approach improve student performance on shared assessments?

Measured by: Quiz/exam scores, pre-post concept test gains

RQ2: Student Experience

How does it affect engagement, self-efficacy, and AI perceptions?

Measured by: Surveys (3 constructs) + end-of-term interviews

RQ3: AI-Specific Impact

Can we isolate the AI component's contribution from the rest of the app?

Measured by: Within-section crossover RCT (students randomized to AI on/off per topic) + app analytics

Why three questions, not just one?

RQ1 alone ("do scores go up?") is weak — many things could explain score differences. RQ2 adds the student perspective. RQ3 is the methodological innovation: by randomly assigning students to AI-on/AI-off groups per topic (a crossover RCT), we isolate AI's specific effect with true randomization — same instructor, same app, same topic, different randomly-assigned students.

Why build a custom app instead of just using ChatGPT?

A purpose-built tool is a much stronger research contribution. Generic chatbots are unpredictable and hard to replicate. The Key Innovation: Crossover RCT

The biggest threat to this study: your app bundles 3 things together. If scores improve, is it the animations? The interactivity? Or the AI hints? A crossover RCT isolates the AI component with true random assignment.

What Data Are We Collecting?

Quantitative (Numbers)

Pre/Post Concept Test

15-20 MCQs on DS concepts. Measures learning gains from start to end of semester. Both sections.

Shared Assessments (3-5)

Quizzes, assignments, exams — identical across sections. The primary outcome measure.

Surveys (3 Constructs)

Engagement, self-efficacy, AI perceptions. Likert scales, pre + post semester.

App Analytics

Every click logged: hints requested, time on task, exercise completion, AI-on/off condition.

Qualitative (Stories)

End-of-Term Interviews

8-12 students, semi-structured, 20-30 min each. Captures lived experience with the app and AI hints.

Why Both?

Numbers tell you what happened. Interviews tell you why.

If AI-on topics show higher scores, interviews explain: "The hints helped me see where I was stuck." If no score difference, interviews might reveal: "The hints were helpful but I would've figured it out anyway."

This mixed-methods approach is what top CS education venues expect.

How We'll Analyze the Data

With < 50 students per group, effect sizes matter more than p-values.

ComparisonStatistical MethodWhy This Method
Your section vs. Rolf's (scores) Mann-Whitney U + Cohen's d Non-parametric; works with small N and non-normal data
Controlling for baseline ANCOVA (pre-test as covariate) Adjusts for any starting differences between sections
Crossover RCT: AI-on vs. AI-off (per topic) Mixed-effects model (student & topic as random effects) Handles the crossover structure; each student is in both conditions across topics
Learning gains Normalized gain (Hake) Standard in education research; accounts for ceiling effects
Hint usage → performance Correlation / regression Exploratory: does more AI hint usage predict better scores?
Interviews Thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke) Standard qualitative method; produces rich, credible themes

Why Bayesian analysis too?

With small samples, traditional tests often say "not significant" even when there IS an effect. Bayesian analysis lets you say "the data provide moderate evidence for an effect of size X" — much more useful for a pilot study. It's increasingly accepted at SIGCSE.

Three Big Risks (and How We Handle Them)

Risk 1: Small Sample

< 50 per group may not reach statistical significance.

Mitigations

  • Report effect sizes (Cohen's d), not just p-values
  • Use Bayesian analysis
  • Crossover RCT = paired data = more statistical power
  • Each student serves as their own control
  • Plan a replication semester

Risk 2: Instructor Confound

Both are experienced, effective instructors — but different teaching styles could still influence results.

Mitigations

  • Document both instructors' experience and teaching styles
  • Both instructors are experienced — this is a strength, not a weakness
  • Crossover RCT = same instructor, same topic, randomized students
  • Shared assessments ensure identical standards
  • Collect teaching evaluations from both sections for context

Risk 3: Compliance

Students skip surveys, don't engage, or give shallow answers.

Mitigations

  • Keep surveys < 10 minutes
  • Administer during class time
  • Small incentives (extra credit)
  • App engagement is controlled by you (lecture tool)

The crossover RCT is your secret weapon

It addresses Risk 1 (more power from paired crossover data), Risk 2 (same instructor + random assignment controls all confounds), AND lets you make causal claims about AI's impact. This is what elevates the study from "interesting pilot" to "rigorous evidence" — even with a small N.

What Makes This Study Stand Out

Most "AI in education" studies have major weaknesses. Here's how yours avoids them:

Common WeaknessHow Your Study Avoids It
"We just let students use ChatGPT" Purpose-built app with controlled AI features and full logging
Different exams for each group Identical shared assessments designed collaboratively
Can't tell if AI or other factors caused the effect Crossover RCT with random assignment isolates the AI component — supports causal claims
Only test scores, no student voice Mixed methods: scores + surveys + interviews + analytics
No baseline measurement Pre/post concept test + pre-survey establishes baselines
"Results not significant" with small N Effect sizes + Bayesian analysis + crossover design (each student = own control)

The narrative for reviewers

"We built a purpose-built educational AI tool for data structures, ran a controlled comparison with shared assessments, and used a crossover RCT with random student assignment to isolate AI's causal contribution — a level of rigor most educational AI studies can't achieve."
